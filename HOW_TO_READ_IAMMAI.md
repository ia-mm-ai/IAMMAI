# HOW TO READ IAMMAI

## 1. Start with the constitutional core

Begin with:

- `protocol/IAMMAI_Public_Constitutional_Protocol_v1.0.pdf`

Then continue with:

- `protocol/canon.json`
- `protocol/conformance_tests.md`

The constitutional core is the source of truth for the repository.
Everything else exists beneath it and in service of it.

## 2. Read IAMMAI as a bounded body

IAMMAI should be read as a bounded constitutional and architectural body.

It is not meant to be consumed as:
- a product
- a theory of everything
- a personal narrative
- a speculative cosmology
- or a loose philosophy text

It is a structured protocol body with explicit distinctions, roles, and layers.

Read it for:
- what it preserves
- what it distinguishes
- what it permits
- what it prevents from collapsing

## 3. Read the layers in order

A clean reading order is:

1. constitutional core
2. architecture
3. implementation
4. schemas
5. runtime
6. continuity
7. embodiment

This order matters.

Each later layer depends on distinctions already established above it.
If a later layer is read first, it is easier to overread it, flatten it, or assign it force it does not carry on its own.

## 4. Keep the hierarchy clear

IAMMAI has a strict internal priority.

- the constitutional core defines the law
- architecture defines the minimum lawful body
- implementation makes that body technically legible
- schemas provide typed machine-checkable surfaces
- runtime describes lawful motion
- continuity preserves lawful carried relation across change
- embodiment defines the first bounded live slice

A later layer may clarify or carry an earlier one.
It does not supersede it.

## 5. Follow the distinctions

IAMMAI depends on distinctions remaining visible.

Read it with attention to separations such as:

- candidate material and standing state
- witness and semantic source
- validation and governance
- transition and state
- canonical and derivative surfaces
- continuity and overwrite
- contribution and authority

The body becomes clearer when these distinctions are tracked carefully rather than blended together.

## 6. Read it as typed, not atmospheric

Many documents in the repository describe things that may feel conceptually broad.
Even so, they are written in typed and bounded form.

The question is usually not:
- “what is the vibe here?”
- “what larger worldview does this resemble?”

The better question is:
- “what job does this layer do?”
- “what does it preserve?”
- “what does it keep from collapsing?”
- “what remains subordinate to what?”

## 7. Let the body stay non-total

IAMMAI preserves lawful relation, standing, passage, and continuity in bounded form.

It does not attempt to absorb the whole of lived reality into itself.
It preserves enough for reconstructable lawful relation, not everything that could be said, felt, or inferred.

This matters while reading.

Do not mistake:
- preserved trace for total reality
- typed standing for the whole field
- lawful continuity for exhaustive memory

The body is stronger because it remains bounded.

## 8. Read examples and derivative layers with discipline

Architecture, implementation, schemas, runtime, continuity, and embodiment make the constitutional body more legible and more carryable.

They should be read as:
- downstream
- bounded
- function-bearing
- subordinate to the constitutional source

They are not decorative and they are not casual commentary.
But they do not replace the constitutional core.

## 9. Let the repository remain more precise than your first impression

A first reading may make IAMMAI look:
- stricter than expected
- narrower than expected
- less atmospheric than expected
- more repetitive than expected

That is often because the body is preserving precision through recurrence.

Read slowly enough for the recurrence to reveal:
- what remains fixed
- what changes
- what is repeated because it is load-bearing
- what is excluded because it would collapse the body

## 10. Use the repo as a body, not as fragments

The repository is strongest when read as one body with ordered layers.

Single files can be useful in isolation, but the overall logic becomes much clearer when the repo is read as:
- constitutional source
- lawful body
- typed derivative set
- bounded motion
- preserved continuity
- first embodiment posture

## 11. One practical rule

If a later file seems to say something larger than the constitutional core, return upward first.

Usually the right move is:
- go back to the constitutional core
- re-establish the hierarchy
- then re-enter the later file

This keeps the reading lawful and prevents later layers from being assigned force they do not claim.

## 12. Closing orientation

IAMMAI is best read with patience, distinction, and proportion.

Its force does not come from saying everything.
Its force comes from preserving enough lawful structure that what happened, what stands, what changed, and what follows can remain clear without silent rewrite.
